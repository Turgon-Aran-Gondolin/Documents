\section{Introduction}
Renormalization and effective theory can be used for non-relativistic quantum mechanics,for Schrodinger equation,according to Lepage\cite{Lepage}.

The basic idea is to mimic the high-energy physics we yet don't know from low-energy data.First,we introduce a momentum cutoff $\Lambda$ as the renormalization process in QFT.This cutoff preserve only those physics that we know of.Then we add local corrections,and use low energy data to fit it.
\section{Constructing a effective theory for Schrodinger equation}
\subsection{The ``true" Theory}
Starting with a simple Coulomb potential,we attach a short-distance potential to it.
\begin{eqnarray}
  H&=&\frac{\vb{p}^2}{2m}+V(r)\\
  \text{where \qquad}V(r)&=&-\frac{\alpha}{r}+V_s(r)
\end{eqnarray}
The exact form of this short-distance potential in Lepage's paper is unknown.With Lepage's original data of one of his figures,I have this potential as my short distance potential:
\begin{equation}
  V_s(r)=-\frac{1.04152e^{-0.9991r}}{r}
\end{equation}
We choose $\alpha=1$ and $m=1$,so the fullform of my potential is
\begin{equation}
  V(r)=-\frac{1}{r}-\frac{1.04152e^{-0.9991r}}{r}
\end{equation}
Solve it and we get

\subsection{Try delta function and perturbation theory}
We can choose delta function $\delta^3(\vb{r})$ to approximate the unknown short-distance potential.The Hamiltonian then is
\begin{equation}
  H_{app}=\frac{\vb{p}^2}{2m}+V(r)+c\delta^3(\vb{r})
\end{equation}
Using 1st-order perturbation theory and we have the binding energy
\begin{equation}\label{e2n}
  E_{2n}=-\frac{1}{2n^2}+c\;\frac{\delta_{l,0}}{\sqrt{\pi}\;n^3}
\end{equation}
The idea is to fit \eqref{e2n} to the lowest energy we have (in our case,it's the 20S binding energy).Here I calculated based on both Lepage's binding energy and my own data,which respectively give $c=-.596255$ and $c=-.619584$.The relative error is given in figure \ref{LepageFigure1}.

But this delta function and perturbation approximation blows up when we ask for more accuracy.The 2nd-order perturbation
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{m\neq n}\frac{\mel{n}{c\delta^3(\vb{r})}{m}\mel{m}{c\delta^3(\vb{r})}{n}}{E_n-E_m}
\end{equation}
obviously gives a divergent term when the scattering momentum $\vb{p}\rightarrow\infty$.The delta function is too singular to be meaningful beyond 1st-order perturbation theory.

Another problem is,we can Fourier transform $V_s$ into momentum space and Taylor expand it.The first term can be transformed back and becomes a delta function.The 2nd term is the Laplacian of delta function.It seems we can get another correction,but the first order perturbation of the 2nd term $\mel{n}{\laplacian{\delta^3(\vb{r})} }{n}$ is again too singular to have any physical meaning.
\subsection{Constructing effective theory}
Now we construct a effective theory in the guidance of renormalization theory (using potential model,of course).This effective theory is specified by this Hamiltonian
\begin{equation}
  H_{eff}=\frac{\vb{p}^2}{2m}+V_{eff}(\vb{r})
\end{equation}
\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=5.2in]{Test_LepageFigure_1.eps}
  \caption{Relative errors in S-wave binding energy plotted versus binding energy for the Coulomb theory,the two curves upper are for only the Coulomb theory and the other two are for $E_{2n}$.}\label{LepageFigure1}
\end{figure}
First we Fourier transform the Coulomb potential $\displaystyle\frac{1}{r}$ into $\displaystyle\frac{4\pi}{q^2}$.Then we introduce a cutoff $\displaystyle\Lambda=\frac{1}{a}$.Potential in momentum space becomes $\displaystyle\frac{4\pi}{q^2}e^{-q^2a^2/2}$.Fourier transform it back into coordinate space and we have the first term of our effective potential $\displaystyle-\frac{erf\pqty{\frac{r}{\sqrt{2}a}}}{r}$ where $\displaystyle erf (z)=\frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi }}\int _0^z\dd t\;e^{-t^2}$.

Now we add local corrections.First we ``smear" the delta function to make it less singular.Define ``smeared" delta function by
\begin{equation}
  \delta_a^3(\vb{r})=\frac{e^{-\frac{r^2}{2a^2}}}{(2\pi)^{\frac{3}{2}}\;a^3}.
\end{equation}
The effective potential with $\mathcal{O}(a^2)$ is
\begin{equation}\label{Veffa2}
  V_{eff}^{(a^2)}=-\frac{\alpha}{r}\;erf\pqty{\frac{r}{\sqrt{2}a}}+c\; a^2\;\delta_a^3(\vb{r})+\mathcal{O}(a^2)
\end{equation}
then.Similarily,the effective potential with $\mathcal{O}(a^4)$ is
\begin{equation}\label{Veffa4}
  V_{eff}^{(a^4)}=-\frac{\alpha}{r}\;erf\pqty{\frac{r}{\sqrt{2}a}}+c\; a^2\;\delta_a^3(\vb{r})+d_1\;a^4\;\laplacian{\delta_a^3(\vb{r})}+d_2\;a^4\;\div{\delta_a^3(\vb{r})}\grad+\mathcal{O}(a^4)£¬
\end{equation}
Using phase shift in $E=10^{-10}$ and $E=10^{-5}$,we can determine the values of c and d in both effective potential.We then have $c^{(a^2)}=-44.294$,$c^{(a^4)}=-39.9477$ and ${d_1}^{(a^4)}=3.26552$.Note that in S-wave,${d_2}^{(a^4)}=0$,and here we just choose $a=1$,we'll discuss why in the following section.

\subsection{Misconceptions}
Firstly,the effective theory can never be the true theory.The odd that we happen to choose the true theory as our effective theory is just too small.

Secondly,the Fourier transform of our effective theory $V_{eff}$ can't converge to that of the true theory.
\clearpage

In my effective theory,the potentials in coordinate space are
\begin{eqnarray}
% \nonumber % Remove numbering (before each equation)
  V(\vb{r}) &=& -\frac{1}{r}-\frac{1.04152 e^{-0.9991 r}}{r} \\
  V_{eff}^{(a^2)}(\vb{r}) &=& -\frac{\text{erf}\left(\frac{r}{\sqrt{2}}\right)}{r}-2.81241 e^{-\frac{r^2}{2}} \\

\section{Using effective theory in perturbative theory}
Effective theory can be used to understand those high-energy physics that we have no knowledge of,and it can be used to simplify calculation of low energy behavior as well.

We use only simple Coulomb potential,
\begin{equation}\label{CoulombSmall}
  H=\frac{\vb{p}^2}{2m}-\frac{\alpha}{r}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha =0.01$,$m=100$,and the effective theory is
\begin{equation}\label{CoulombSmall}

\begin{eqnarray}\label{Heff}
  f_{eff}^{(1)}(\vb{q})&=&-\frac{4\pi\alpha}{q^2}e^{-q^2a^2/2}(1+cq^2a^2/2)\\
  &=&-\frac{4\pi\alpha}{q^2}£¨1+£¨c-1)q^2a^2/2+\mathcal{O}(q^4a^4))
\end{eqnarray}

When calculating 2nd-order Born approximation
\begin{equation}
  T=V+V\frac{1}{E_i-E_m+i\hbar\epsilon}V
\end{equation}
we have
\begin{equation}
  V\ket{\psi^{(+)}}=T\ket{\vb{k}}=V\ket{\vb{k}}+V\frac{1}{E_i-E_m+i\hbar\epsilon}V\ket{\vb{k}}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{eqnarray}
% \nonumber % Remove numbering (before each equation)
  f(\vb{k_0},\vb{k'}) &=& (2\pi)^3\mel{\vb{k'}}{V}{\psi^{(+)}}\\
   &=& (2\pi)^3(\mel{\vb{k'}}{V}{\vb{k_0}}+\mel{\vb{k'}}{V\frac{1}{E_i-E_m+i\hbar\epsilon}V}{\vb{k_0}}) \\
   &=& f^{(1)}(\vb{k_0},\vb{k'})+f^{(2)}(\vb{k_0},\vb{k'})
\end{eqnarray}
$f^{(1)}(\vb{k_0},\vb{k'})$ as above,and$f^{(2)}(\vb{k_0},\vb{k'})$ can be the integration form of $f^{(1)}(\vb{k_0},\vb{k'})$.
\begin{eqnarray}
  f^{(2)}(\vb{k_0},\vb{k'})&=&(2\pi)^3\mel{\vb{k'}}{V\frac{1}{E_i-E_m+i\hbar\epsilon}V}{\vb{k_0}} \\
  &=&\int\frac{\dd^3k''}{(2\pi)^3}f^{(1)}(\vb{k''},\vb{k'})\frac{1}{E_i-E_m+i\hbar\epsilon}f^{(1)}(\vb{k_0},\vb{k''})\\
  &=&\int\frac{\dd^3k''}{(2\pi)^3}f^{(1)}(\vb{k''},\vb{k'})\frac{2m}{k_0^2-k''^2+2mi\hbar\epsilon}f^{(1)}(\vb{k_0},\vb{k''})
\end{eqnarray}
Assuming $\vb{k}=\vb{k_0}-\vb{k''}$,and $\vb{k}-\vb{q}=\vb{k'}-\vb{k''}$,redefine $\epsilon$ as $2m\epsilon$,and
\begin{equation}
  f^{(2)}=\int\frac{\dd^3k}{(2\pi)^3}f^{(1)}(\vb{k}-\vb{q})\frac{2m}{k_0^2-(\vb{k}-\vb{k_0})^2+i\hbar\epsilon}f^{(1)}(\vb{k})
\end{equation}
For the 1st-order,the ``true" theory is almost identical to the effective one.In the 2nd-order,the difference in scattering amplitude of these two is mainly contribute by $f^{(2)}$ and $f_{eff}^{(2)}$,and $p$ and $q$ can be ignored comparing to $k$.
\begin{eqnarray}
% \nonumber % Remove numbering (before each equation)
  f_{eff}^{(2)}-f^{(2)} &=& \int\frac{\dd^3k}{(2\pi)^3}\bqty{\pqty{\frac{-4\pi\alpha}{k^2}\frac{-2m}{k^2}\frac{-4\pi\alpha}{k^2}}e^{-k^2a^2}(1+k^2a^2/2)^2-\pqty{\frac{-4\pi\alpha}{k^2}\frac{-2m}{k^2}\frac{-4\pi\alpha}{k^2}}} \\
   &=& \frac{10}{3} \sqrt{\pi } \alpha^2  a^3 m
\end{eqnarray}
making correction to c
\begin{equation}
  -\frac{4\pi\alpha}{q^2}(c-1)q^2a^2/2=-\frac{10}{3} \sqrt{\pi } \alpha^2  a^3 m
\end{equation}
and we have $\displaystyle c=1+\frac{5}{3\sqrt{\pi}}\alpha a m=1.00940316$.
